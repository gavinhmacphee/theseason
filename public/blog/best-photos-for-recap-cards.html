How to Pick the Best Photos for Your Season Recap Card

Feb 16, 2026 · 3 min read

Action Shots Beat Posed Photos

That team photo where everyone's lined up in two rows? It's fine for the fridge. But for a recap card, you want the photos that actually feel like the season - the ones with movement, intensity, and personality.

An action shot of your kid in the middle of a play says more about their season than any posed portrait ever will. It doesn't need to be perfectly framed. It just needs to capture them in their element.

Look for Emotion

The best recap card photos have feeling in them. Scroll through your camera roll and look for these moments:

  • Celebrations - fist pumps after a big play, running to the sideline, team huddles
  • High-fives - with teammates, coaches, or you on the sideline
  • Focus faces - that look of concentration right before the play starts
  • Post-game exhaustion - water bottle in hand, big grin, still in uniform

These are the photos that trigger real memories. Not "we had a team," but "I remember how it felt to play on that team."

Team Photos Still Matter

Don't skip the team photo entirely. It's great for context - it shows who was on the team, what the jerseys looked like, and how small (or tall) your kid was that season. Use it as a secondary image alongside a stronger action shot.

Phone Photos Work Fine

You do not need a professional photographer. The photos you took from the sideline on your phone are perfect. Most parents have dozens of decent shots from any given season - they just never do anything with them.

A few quick tips for picking the right one:

  • Crop tight on your player. If they're small in the frame, crop in. You'll lose some resolution but gain a much stronger image.
  • Avoid blurry or dark shots. A slightly grainy photo is fine, but if you can't tell who's in it, keep scrolling.
  • Golden hour games look amazing. If your kid played any evening games with that warm sunset light, those photos almost always look great.

It Doesn't Need to Be From the Best Game

Here's something parents get stuck on: they want the photo to be from the championship game or the game where their kid had three big plays. But the best photo isn't always from the best game.

The photo needs to capture who your kid was that season. Sometimes that's a Tuesday practice scrimmage where they were laughing with their best friend. Sometimes it's a rainy game where they looked like they'd been through a mud run. Those photos have character.

Pick the one that makes you feel something when you look at it. That's the right one.
